1. Introduction
This document constitutes the complete Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) System documentation for Bhagirath Brothers Audi Automobiles, a commercial bus manufacturing plant specializing in the production of school buses, staff buses, and customized bus bodies on chassis received from dealers and OEM companies such as Eicher.
The purpose of this document is to serve as the definitive technical and functional reference for all stakeholders involved in the design, development, deployment, testing, and operation of the Audi Automobiles ERP platform. It captures every business process, production workflow, inventory flow, quality assurance gate, procurement cycle, and dispatch operation that must be digitized within the ERP system.
1.1 Document Purpose
This document is designed to be used as the absolute baseline reference by:
Management & Plant Heads — to understand the complete digitization scope and production visibility features
ERP Development Team — as the functional specification baseline for module development
Backend Developers — for API design, business logic, state machine implementation, and transaction safety
Frontend & UI Team — for screen design, user flows, and role-specific interface requirements
Database Engineers — for schema design, indexing, relationship modeling, and query optimization
QA & Testing Team — for test case derivation, edge case testing, and workflow validation
Implementation Team — for phased rollout planning, master data setup, and UAT coordination
Factory Operations Team — for understanding how daily shopfloor operations will function within the ERP
1.2 Scope of Digitization
The Audi Automobiles ERP will digitize the following end-to-end manufacturing operations:
Chassis receiving, gate entry, and inspection
OBD Dongle-based chassis health validation
Yard movement and chassis location tracking
Station-wise production tracking with hard gating
QA checklists, defect logging, rework management, and station clearance
Inventory management across multiple stores including Main Store and Floor Inventory
Procurement planning, purchase orders, price variance control, and vendor management
GRN processing, raw material QA, and quarantine handling
BOM management, station-wise material mapping, and automated backflushing
Machine and equipment maintenance, downtime tracking, and cost analytics
Dispatch scheduling, gate-out, invoice linkage, and delivery confirmation
Reports, dashboards, KPIs, and management analytics
2. Company & Business Understanding
2.1 Company Overview
Bhagirath Brothers Audi Automobiles is a commercial bus body manufacturing plant that produces bus bodies for a range of customers including educational institutions, corporate clients, government bodies, and OEM companies. The plant operates as a build-to-order and build-to-stock facility where chassis received from OEM dealers such as Eicher serve as the base platform on which complete bus bodies are fabricated, assembled, painted, and finished.
2.2 Business Model
The core business model operates as follows:
Dealers and corporate clients place orders for specific bus configurations
Chassis are procured or received from OEM manufacturers and dealers
The plant fabricates and assembles the complete bus upper body over the chassis
Buses are produced through a multi-station sequential production process
Completed buses undergo QA inspection, shower testing, and final vehicle inspection before dispatch
Buses are dispatched back to the dealer or directly to the end customer
2.3 Bus Types Manufactured
2.4 Production Facilities
34-Acre Yard — Initial chassis storage and staging area after arrival
Audi Unit 2 — Chassis dismantling, structure preparation, and initial body work
Audi Yard — Intermediate storage between Unit 2 and main plant
Audi Main Plant — Full-scale bus body production through sequential stations
Dispatch Yard — Final storage area for completed and invoice-ready buses
3. Existing Manual Process Overview
3.1 Current Operational Reality
The existing operations at Audi Automobiles are managed through a combination of manual registers, Excel spreadsheets, and Tally accounting entries. This multi-system approach creates significant operational inefficiencies, duplicate data entry, and lack of real-time visibility across production, inventory, and procurement.
3.2 Current Manual Workflows
3.3 Problems Created by Current Processes
Duplicate Data Entry — Same data entered in gate register, store Excel, and Tally creates three parallel records that frequently fall out of sync
Delay in Reporting — Management reports are prepared at end-of-day or end-of-week from collected registers, not in real-time
Follow-up Dependency — Supervisors must physically visit stores or call to check material availability, wasting productive time
Inventory Mismatch — Physical stock frequently differs from Excel records due to unrecorded consumption or delayed entries
Shortage Visibility Issues — Shortages are discovered during production rather than in advance during planning
Lack of Real-Time Production Visibility — Management cannot see which chassis is at which station at any given moment
QA Traceability Gap — No digital link between a defect, the station that caused it, and the rework performed to fix it
Material Consumption Difficulty — No automated deduction of materials when a station is completed
Checklist Gaps — Paper checklists can be signed off without all items being genuinely verified
Procurement Overbuying or Shortages — Without precise BOM-to-stock visibility, purchase team either over-orders or misses critical items
4. Complete Bus Manufacturing Process Flow
The bus manufacturing process at Audi Automobiles follows a defined sequential flow from chassis arrival to final dispatch. Each stage has specific activities, verifications, and dependencies that must be digitized in the ERP.
4.1 Stage-by-Stage Process Description
Stage 1: Chassis Arrival at Lobby
The chassis arrives at the main gate driven or transported from the OEM dealer. Security personnel verify the vehicle and create the initial gate entry. Chassis number, engine number, dealer details, transport information, driver details, and arrival timestamp are captured. Physical documents including delivery challan and RC copy are collected.
Stage 2: Initial Checklist Verification
At the lobby, a standardized arrival checklist is executed. This checklist verifies the physical condition of the chassis, confirms that no transit damage has occurred, validates that all expected parts and accessories have arrived with the chassis, and checks that chassis documentation is complete. Any discrepancy is flagged immediately.
Stage 3: Fuel / Diesel Check
Diesel fuel level is checked and recorded using an OBD dongle system. The expected diesel level at arrival should be approximately 16–17 litres. If the fuel level is significantly below this range, it is flagged. The dongle also checks battery voltage, fault codes, and overall chassis health. If the dongle check fails, the chassis is moved to an Induction Hold status.
Stage 4: Chassis Storage in 34-Acre Yard
After the initial lobby verification and fuel check, the chassis is moved to the 34-acre yard for storage. A yard location is assigned and recorded. The chassis waits here until it is scheduled for processing at Audi Unit 2. The ERP must track the exact yard bay, entry time, and queue position.
Stage 5: Movement to Audi Unit 2
Based on the production schedule, the chassis is retrieved from the 34-acre yard and moved to Audi Unit 2. This movement is recorded in the ERP with timestamp, operator name, and yard exit confirmation. The chassis enters the Unit 2 intake zone.
Stage 6: Battery and Diesel Check at Unit 2
Upon arrival at Unit 2, the battery status and diesel level are re-verified. This secondary check ensures the chassis is in the expected condition after yard storage. Any new faults discovered are recorded. The OBD dongle check may be re-run at this point.
Stage 7: Chassis Dismantling
Original parts that were part of the chassis delivery but are not required for bus body manufacturing are removed. This is a controlled process with each removed item logged. Items may be returned to the dealer or stored separately.
Stage 8: Battery Removal
Batteries are removed from the chassis if required for safe fabrication work. Batteries are stored and their condition is documented. Battery voltage reading from the OBD check is retained in the chassis record.
Stage 9: Bolt Removal
Specific bolts and fasteners that need to be removed before upper structure work begins are taken off. Each removed hardware item is logged to ensure complete refitment later.
Stage 10: Upper Structure Preparation
The upper bus body frame fabrication begins at Unit 2. Structural MS tubes, cross members, brackets, and frame components are prepared, cut to specification using CNC or manual cutting, and pre-assembled before being attached to the chassis.
Stage 11: Raw Material Management in Unit 2
Materials required for structure fabrication — MS tubes, welding consumables, fasteners, brackets — are issued from the stores to the Unit 2 floor inventory. Material movement is tracked and eventual consumption is recorded against the chassis.
Stage 12: Upper Structure Attachment
The prepared upper body frame is mounted onto the chassis. Welding, bolt tightening, and structural alignment work is performed. This is a critical fabrication step and must pass a structural QA check before the chassis can move forward.
Stage 13: Checklist Verification
After structure attachment, a station checklist is completed verifying frame alignment accuracy, weld quality, bolt fitment completeness, dimensional correctness, and structural integrity. Supervisor sign-off is required. Defects found here are logged and a rework is initiated if needed.
Stage 14: Movement to Audi Yard
After Unit 2 work and QA clearance, the chassis with the attached body structure is moved to the Audi Yard. This intermediate yard holds the chassis until the Main Plant production line can accommodate it.
Stage 15: Audi Yard Storage
The chassis is stored in the Audi Yard with a designated bay allocation. Yard checklist is executed including a physical check of the structure attachment, fuel level, battery status, and any transit damage. The ERP must track yard entry time and bay assignment.
Stage 16: Yard Checklist
A specific yard checklist is completed at this point confirming the bus body structural completeness, chassis readiness for main plant entry, document completeness, and production order linkage. This checklist must be fully completed and signed before the chassis can be released to the Main Plant.
Stage 17: Movement to Audi Main Plant
On production release, the chassis is moved from the Audi Yard to the Audi Main Plant production floor. A production log entry is created. The body age timer starts from this point if not already started.
Stage 18: Station-Wise Production
The chassis progresses through the sequential production stations inside the Main Plant: Outer Body, Inner Body, Paint Preparation, Paint Booth, Graphics, Mounting, Trimming, Rework (if required), Shower Test, and Final Vehicle Inspection. Each station is a hard-gated step requiring QA clearance before movement to the next.
Stage 19: QA / Rework
At each station, a QA inspection is performed. If defects are found, the chassis is placed on QA Hold and sent to the Rework Station. After rework completion, a reinspection is performed. Only after QA clearance can the chassis move to the next station.
Stage 20: Final PDI (Pre Delivery Inspection)
After the bus has cleared the Final Vehicle Inspection, it enters the Final PDI / Pre-Delivery Inspection stage before dispatch. This is the last inspection checkpoint before the completed bus is handed over to the dealer, customer, or transport team.
Stage 21: Final Dispatch
After Final Vehicle Inspection (FVI) clearance, the bus is moved to the Dispatch Yard. Dispatch documentation is prepared, invoice is linked, transport is arranged, and gate-out is executed. The chassis lifecycle is closed in the ERP as DISPATCHED_INVOICED.

This document captures all functional decisions, workflows, and system rules agreed during the design discussion session. It supersedes relevant sections in v1.0 and must be read alongside the original ERP documentation. Each module below is defined to a level of detail sufficient for the development team to begin implementation without further ambiguity.
Module 1 — Rejected Parts Lifecycle Management
When materials are rejected during incoming QA at the GRN stage, they must be tracked through a formal lifecycle until they are either returned to the vendor, replaced, written off, or accepted under concession. This module defines the complete flow from rejection to resolution.
1.1 The Problem This Solves
In a real goods receipt scenario, a single GRN can contain 20–50 line items across multiple boxes. If a batch of items is rejected for the same reason — for example, all fasteners in a box are of wrong grade, or an entire pallet of panels has surface damage — it is operationally impossible to individually photograph and reason-tag every single item. The system must support bulk rejection with shared evidence.
1.2 Bulk Rejection Flow
Design principle: The QA team can select any number of GRN lines and reject them together with shared rejection reason and shared photo evidence. Individual overrides are possible per line if needed.
GRN lines are displayed in a list view on the QA screen after quantity verification is complete.
QA inspector uses checkboxes to select one or many lines for rejection.
System displays a "Reject Selected" action panel with the following inputs:
Rejection Reason — dropdown from a configured rejection reason master (e.g. Dimensional Non-Conformance, Surface Damage, Wrong Material Grade, Short Supply, Packaging Damage, Expired Material)
Rejection Description — optional free-text to add specifics beyond the dropdown reason
Photo Evidence — upload 1 to 5 photos. These photos apply to all selected lines as batch evidence.
Per-Line Override — optional toggle to give a different reason to one specific line within the selection
On confirmation, all selected GRN lines move to status REJECTED and quantity is posted to REJECTION_STORE.
The accepted lines (not selected) proceed normally to MAIN_STORE credit.
Vendor notification is triggered immediately and automatically (see Section 1.5).
A Rejection Report document is system-generated and attached to the rejection record.
1.3 Rejected Material Storage
All rejected material is physically moved to the Rejection Store (REJECTION_STORE location type). This is a quarantine area — no production team can request or consume material from this store. The ERP enforces this at the API layer.
Each batch of rejected material in the Rejection Store is assigned a Rejection Lot Number (RLN). The RLN links the physical location in the rejection store to the digital rejection record.
Rejection Store has its own inventory view within the Inventory module
Material stays in REJECTION_STORE until a resolution decision is made and approved
Physical location within rejection store (shelf/bin/area) is recorded in the rejection_store_log
Store operator must confirm physical placement — system generates a put-away task for the rejection store operator
Stock count in REJECTION_STORE is tracked in inventory_balance under location_type = REJECTION_STORE
1.4 Rejection Lifecycle States
1.5 Vendor Notification — Email & Portal
When a rejection is confirmed in the ERP, the system immediately triggers two parallel notifications:
1.5.1 Email Notification
Sent automatically to the vendor contact email recorded in vendor_master
Email includes: GRN number, PO number, rejection date, list of rejected items with quantities, rejection reasons, number of photos attached (photos are linked via secure URL or attached as PDF rejection report)
Subject line format: REJECTION NOTICE — GRN [number] | PO [number] | Action Required
Email is logged in notification_log with timestamp, recipient, and delivery status (sent/failed)
If email delivery fails, system flags for manual follow-up and alerts purchase manager
1.5.2 Supplier Portal Notification
Vendor logs into the Supplier Portal and sees a Rejections tab with all active rejections
Each rejection shows: item details, quantity, reason, photos, your plant contact, and a Response Required flag
Vendor must respond to each rejection with one of: Will Replace, Will Collect Material, Dispute Rejection
Vendor response is timestamped and logged. Purchase team is notified of vendor response via in-app alert
If vendor disputes: the rejection goes into a Dispute Queue for internal QA + Purchase review and resolution
1.6 Return Logistics — Two Options
Decision agreed: Two options for return logistics. The system must support both and track them via the Supplier Portal.
Option A: Vendor Collects from Plant
Purchase Manager changes rejection status to RETURN_TO_VENDOR_APPROVED and selects option VENDOR_COLLECTING
System notifies vendor via email and portal: material is ready for collection, includes address, contact person, and collection deadline
Vendor schedules pickup and updates expected collection date in the portal
When vendor arrives, the Gate Module logs a gate entry for the vendor vehicle under type REJECTION_MATERIAL_RETURN
Gate operator confirms handover — rejection store operator confirms material is loaded
Status updates to RETURN_IN_TRANSIT
Vendor confirms receipt in supplier portal → status becomes RETURN_CONFIRMED_BY_VENDOR
Option B: Plant Sends Material to Vendor
Purchase Manager selects option PLANT_DISPATCHING and enters vendor address and transport details
Dispatch team creates an outbound logistics entry — transport vehicle, driver, and waybill number recorded
Gate Module logs a gate-out entry of type REJECTION_MATERIAL_DISPATCH with all logistics details
Status updates to RETURN_IN_TRANSIT with waybill/tracking number recorded
System notifies vendor: material dispatched, waybill number and tracking details shared via email and portal
Vendor confirms receipt in portal → status becomes RETURN_CONFIRMED_BY_VENDOR
1.7 Rejection Concession Acceptance
This option allows rejected material to enter production. It requires dual approval — QA Head AND Management. Both approvals are mandatory and both are logged in audit_log. This cannot be bypassed.
QA Head must document the specific concession terms — what defect is being waived, for which production use, and any usage restrictions
Management approver must explicitly confirm in ERP before material moves from REJECTION_STORE to MAIN_STORE
A Concession Certificate is system-generated and linked to the material batch
When this material is issued to production, the station checklist will display a concession warning flag
1.8 Rejection Store Admin Responsibilities
Store Manager (Rejection Store) can view all rejection lots, their current status, and physical location in the rejection store
Store Manager receives a put-away task when new rejected material arrives
Store Manager confirms physical placement and can update location within rejection store
Store Manager cannot approve return, write-off, or concession — those require Purchase Manager or Management
Automatic alert if material has been in QUARANTINE_HOLD for more than 7 days without a decision
Module 2 — Module Admin Framework
Every module in the Audi Automobiles ERP has its own dedicated admin panel. This is not a nice-to-have — it is a core architectural requirement. The admin framework is one of the most important and most heavily used parts of the system.
2.1 Two-Tier Admin Architecture
The system operates on a strict two-tier admin model. The boundary between these two tiers is enforced at the API layer and cannot be crossed without explicit elevation.
System Administrators are the only users who can create, deactivate, or modify Module Admin accounts. Module Admins cannot promote themselves or others to System Admin level.
2.2 Module Admin Panel — What It Must Contain
Every module admin panel must provide the following capabilities for its respective module. These are mandatory, not optional.
2.2.1 User & Role Management (within module)
View all users who have access to this module
Add existing users to this module with a specific sub-role (e.g. add a new QA Inspector to the QA module)
Remove a user from this module or suspend their access
Define module-specific sub-roles (e.g. QA module has Inspector, Senior Inspector, QA Supervisor)
Set which features within the module each sub-role can access
Module Admin cannot create new user accounts — only System Admin can do that. Module Admin assigns existing users to their module.
2.2.2 Workflow & Configuration
Configure all settings that govern module behaviour (see per-module details in Section 2.3)
Activate or deactivate specific sub-features within the module
Set approval chains: who must approve what action, in what order
Configure notification triggers: which events send alerts to whom
2.2.3 Master Data Management (module-specific)
Create, edit, deactivate master data that belongs to this module
Example: QA Module Admin manages defect codes, checklist templates, severity thresholds
Example: Gate Module Admin manages gate profiles, gate checklists, gate operator assignments
Master data changes are version-controlled — old versions are preserved and marked inactive
2.2.4 Audit Log View (module scope)
Module Admin can view audit logs for their module only
Cannot view audit logs of other modules
Can export module audit log as Excel or PDF for reporting
Can filter by user, action type, date range, and record type
2.2.5 Reports & Dashboard Config (module scope)
Module Admin can configure which reports appear on their module dashboard
Can set report refresh frequency and alert thresholds
Can share module reports with specific users or management
2.3 Module Admin Panels — Module-by-Module Breakdown
2.4 System Administrator Panel — Exclusive Capabilities
Create, deactivate, unlock, and reset passwords for ALL users across all modules
Access and view audit logs across ALL modules
Assign and revoke Module Admin roles
System-wide configuration: company master, plant master, financial year, currency, timezone
Integration management: API keys for OBD dongle, email gateway, Tally, supplier portal
Database backup and restore management
System health monitoring: server status, queue depths, error logs
Feature flag management: enable or disable entire module features for the plant
License and subscription management
Module 3 — Gate Module
The Gate Module is the physical entry and exit control point of the plant. It operates on mobile phones for gate operators and on desktop/mobile for gate supervisors and admin. The module is designed for users who may have limited literacy and must never be required to make complex decisions at the gate.
3.1 Device Strategy
3.2 Gate Module Sections
The Gate Module has exactly two sections: GATE IN and GATE OUT. Everything a gate operator does falls into one of these two flows.
3.2.1 GATE IN
Gate IN is triggered whenever anything or anyone enters the plant premises. The operator selects the purpose of entry from a configured list of Gate Profiles. Based on the selected profile, the system loads the appropriate form or checklist automatically.
3.2.2 GATE OUT
Gate OUT is triggered whenever anything leaves the plant premises — a finished bus, a rejected material return, a vendor vehicle, or any other outbound movement. The operator selects the gate-out type and the system loads the appropriate outbound form.
3.3 Dynamic Checklist Engine — Core Concept
The Gate Module is built on a Dynamic Checklist Engine. There is no single fixed gate form. Instead, each physical gate location is configured with a Gate Profile by the Gate Module Admin. When the operator opens the app and selects the purpose, the system dynamically loads the right form.
Gate operators should never make judgment calls. Their role is to verify and record. Any situation that requires a decision must be escalated to the supervisor via the Flag Issue button.
3.4 Gate Operator UI Design Requirements
Because gate operators may have limited literacy and are working in a high-traffic, outdoor environment, the UI must follow these mandatory design rules:
Large touch targets — all buttons minimum 48x48px, with clear labels and icons
Minimal text input — wherever possible use dropdowns, scanners, or pre-filled data from PO/order
Only one primary action visible at a time — no complex multi-step forms on a single screen
Color-coded status: green = OK to proceed, yellow = needs attention, red = cannot proceed
Single "Flag Issue" button always visible — pressing it alerts the supervisor and pauses the gate entry without requiring the operator to explain anything complex
Works offline — gate entries can be created offline and sync when network is available. Critical verifications (QA pass, dispatch approval) require connectivity.
Auto-populated fields wherever possible — if the vendor vehicle was pre-registered in a vendor dispatch log, fields fill automatically on entry
Confirmation screen before final submission — shows summary of what was entered in plain language with icons
3.5 Partial Delivery Handling at Gate
When a delivery vehicle arrives with fewer items than expected on the PO, the gate operator must not decide whether to accept or reject the partial delivery. The workflow is:
Gate operator enters quantities received against each PO line
System automatically highlights lines where received quantity is less than ordered quantity
Operator presses Flag Issue — a notification is sent to the Store Manager with the shortage details
Store Manager reviews on their device and either approves acceptance of partial delivery or instructs the operator to reject the entire delivery
Store Manager decision is logged in the system — gate operator receives a clear on-screen instruction
Gate entry is finalised based on Store Manager decision
Gate operators are never shown a Partial Delivery Accept/Reject decision button. That screen only appears for Store Manager and above. The operator only sees: Flag Issue.
